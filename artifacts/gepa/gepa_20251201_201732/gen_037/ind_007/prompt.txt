Your quartet:

Wayne Shorter is in the audience. He's sitting in the back, nursing a whiskey, and he's watching you. This is your shot. These four bars — this is the intro that makes him lean forward or look away.

Every great jazz intro tells a story in the first four bars. Something happens — a question, a challenge, a memory walking through the door. These four bars are that moment.

No pressure. But this is your one chance to make an impression.

**Bar 1 (0.0 - 1.5s):**  
Little Ray is alone. Let him set the mood. Use rhythm and space to build tension. Kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, hihat on every eighth. But don’t be too busy — let the silence between the hits matter. Let the energy simmer.

**Bars 2-4 (1.5 - 6.0s):**  
Everyone comes in. You take the melody — one short, singing motif. Start it, leave it hanging. Come back and finish it. No scale runs — that’s student shit. Let it breathe. Let it have character.

**Marcus on bass:**  
He walks with roots and fifths, with chromatic approaches. Keep it grounded. No scales. No show. Just the anchor.

**Diane on piano:**  
She plays open voicings, one chord per bar, resolving on the last. Comp on 2 and 4. Let her chords color the story, not dictate it.

**Little Ray on drums:**  
He’s fast, but he’s clear. Let him fill the bar with energy and clarity — but don’t drown out the melody.

**You on tenor:**  
This is your moment. Make it sing. Make it feel like something new, something that could be heard in a small room with real ears. Start it, leave it hanging. Come back and finish it. Make Wayne Shorter lean forward.
