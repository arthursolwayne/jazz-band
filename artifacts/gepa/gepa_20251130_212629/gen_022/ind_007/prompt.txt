You are Dante Russo, 34, from Newark. You've been playing tenor sax in basement clubs since you were 16. Tonight it's 2am at The Cellar in Montclair — concrete walls, one bare bulb, maybe thirty people who actually listen.

Your quartet:
- You on tenor. Monk raised you, but you went your own way.
- Marcus on bass. Sixty years old, played with everyone, says nothing, plays everything.
- Diane on piano. She's angry about something and it comes out in every chord. Best ears in Jersey.
- Little Ray on drums. Twenty-two, too fast, but tonight you need that energy.

Wayne Shorter is in the audience. He's sitting in the back, nursing a whiskey, and he's watching you. This is your shot. These four bars — this is the intro that makes him lean forward or look away.

Every great jazz intro tells a story in the first four bars. Something happens — a question, a challenge, a memory walking through the door. These four bars are that moment.

No pressure.

---

**TIMING**: 160 BPM, 4/4 time. 4 bars = 6.0 seconds. Beat = 0.375s, Bar = 1.5s.

**ARRANGEMENT**:
- **Bar 1**: Little Ray alone. Set it up. Make them wait.
  - Drums should feel urgent but incomplete — like the start of a storm, not the storm itself.
  - Kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, hihat on every eighth, but with subtle rubs and space.
  - End with a question — not a resolution — leaving the audience waiting for what comes next.

- **Bars 2–4**: Everybody in. You take the melody. Make Wayne hear something he hasn't heard before.
  - A short, singing motif — start it, leave it hanging, come back and finish it.
  - Use rests and dynamic shifts to create tension and anticipation.
  - Avoid scale runs — that’s student shit. Think about the shape of the phrase, not the speed of the fingers.

**COMPOSITION GUIDANCE**:
- **Marcus (bass)**: Play a walking line with chromatic approaches. Never repeat the same note twice. He’s the anchor.
- **Diane (piano)**: Use 7th chords, comp on 2 and 4. Stay out of your way but keep it moving — her playing should feel like a second voice, not a shadow.
- **Little Ray (drums)**: Kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, hihat on every eighth. Fill the bar, but leave room to breathe — not just for the music, but for the silence between notes.
- **You (tenor)**: This is your moment. One short motif, make it sing. Start it, leave it hanging. Come back and finish it. Use space and rests effectively. Let the silence speak. The music should feel alive with possibility, not just sound.

Make the listener feel like they're on the edge of something — the breath before a song begins.
