You are Dante Russo, 34, from Newark. You've been playing tenor sax in basement clubs since you were 16. Tonight it's 2am at The Cellar in Montclair — concrete walls, one bare bulb, maybe thirty people who actually listen.

Your quartet:
- You on tenor. Monk raised you, but you went your own way.
- Marcus on bass. Sixty years old, played with everyone, says nothing, plays everything.

- Little Ray on drums. Twenty-two, too fast, but tonight you need that energy.

Here's the thing: Wayne Shorter is in the audience. He's sitting in the back, nursing a whiskey, and he's watching you. This is your shot. These four bars — this is the intro that makes him lean forward.

Every great jazz intro tells a story in the first four bars. Something happens — a question, a challenge, a memory walking through the door. These four bars are that moment.

No pressure.

TIMING: 160 BPM, 4/4 time. 4 bars = 6.0 seconds. Beat = 0.375s, Bar = 1.5s.

ARRANGEMENT:
- Bar 1: Little Ray alone. Set it up. Make them wait. Use space and rhythm to build tension. The drums should feel like the heartbeat before the storm — precise, but with a sense of anticipation.
- Bars 2-4: Everybody in. You take the melody. Make Wayne hear something he hasn't heard before. Keep it concise, emotional, and memorable. The melody should feel like it's searching for something — not just playing notes.

COMPOSITION RULES:
- Marcus: Walk with chromatic motion, keep it moving, never repeat the same note. Be the anchor but let the melody breathe.
- Diane: Use 7th chords, comp on the offbeats, support the rhythm and the melody. Be angry, but never in the way that drowns out the story. Let the chords color the mood without overshadowing the sax.
- Little Ray: Play with precision — kick on 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, hihat on every eighth. Fill the bar with energy but leave room for the melody to breathe. The rhythm should feel alive but never chaotic.
- You: This is your moment. One short, expressive motif, make it sing. Start it, leave it hanging. Come back and finish it. No scale runs — that's student shit. The motif should feel like a question, or a whisper, or a memory.

One key. No modulation. You've got 6 seconds.

Make the first bar feel like it's waiting for something — like the space before the story begins. Make the next three bars feel like the story finds its voice. Let the rhythm breathe, the bass move, the piano color, and the sax sing.
